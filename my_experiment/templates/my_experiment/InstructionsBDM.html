In this part, you will report how much each snack is worth to you. You will do 3 practice rounds first, then you will enter bids for all snacks.

What is a “bid”?

Your bid for a snack should be the maximum amount of money you would be willing to pay to receive that snack. You can enter bids with cents (e.g., 2.75).

How the BDM rule works

After you enter your bid, the computer will draw a random price for a snack. This random price is independent of your bid.

  • If your bid is greater than or equal to the random price, you buy the snack and you pay the random price (not your bid).
  • If your bid is less than the random price, you do not buy the snack and you pay nothing.

Important: why you should bid truthfully

This rule is designed so that your best strategy is to enter your true willingness to pay:

  • If you bid too low, you may miss chances to buy a snack at a low random price that you actually would have wanted.
  • If you bid too high, you might end up buying a snack at a random price that is higher than what it is worth to you.

Because you pay the random price (not your bid), the safest and best approach is: bid the maximum you are truly willing to pay.

What happens in the main task

You will enter bids for all snacks. Then the computer will randomly select one snack to be the binding snack (the only one that is used for the purchase decision). A random price will be drawn for that binding snack, and the buy/no-buy decision will be determined using the rule above.

Practice rounds

In the practice rounds, you will try the same rule with example snacks so you can see how the random price and decision work. Practice rounds are only for learning.
